What is a Bank Bonus?

Banks generate revenue by collecting deposits from customers and lending that money to others. To encourage new customers to switch from their current bank, they often offer attractive cash bonuses as an incentive.

A bank bonus is one of the easiest ways to earn "passive income" because it usually requires no spending at all. You simply sign up for a bank account, meet the requirements, and receive the reward. Most commonly, this involves setting up a direct deposit of a specific amount within a given timeframe.

Example: Fifth Third Bank offered me a $400 bonus for making a single direct deposit of at least $500 within the first 90 days of opening the account.

Here’s are the steps you will follow to start receiving bank bonuses.

  1. Open the account: Most accounts can be opened online, and the process typically takes about 15 minutes. Once completed, you'll receive your new account and routing numbers.
  2. Set up direct deposit: Redirect your workplace direct deposit to the new account. While this might feel tricky the first time, it becomes quick and easy with practice—about 5 minutes once you know how.
  3. Withdraw deposited funds: Once the direct deposit is complete, you can transfer the money out. I recommend leaving at least $5 in the account until you receive the bonus. You can withdraw through ATMs, at the bank, or digitally—my preferred method is linking the debit card to Venmo to transfer funds.
  4. Receive the bonus: Banks pay bonuses at different rates. Some may credit the bonus within a week, while others might take up to 90 days.
  5. Transfer remaining funds: After receiving the bonus, withdraw the rest of your money and spend your reward as you wish. If you used the debit card, consider locking it in the app to prevent unauthorized charges.
  6. Close the account: Once your account balance is $0, most banks will automatically close it after a certain period. Alternatively, you can call to close the account. Be sure to wait at least 90 days post-bonus to avoid repayment requests.

Yes, this process requires some effort, but it's minimal. In my experience, it takes about an hour of work per bonus, and earning $100–$400 for that hour is well worth it.

Over time, the process becomes easier. The most time-consuming steps are setting up the direct deposit and transferring the funds, but these tasks quickly feel routine.

If you have a job, your direct deposit has to go somewhere—why not get paid extra just for where it lands?

Why Do Banks Offer Bonuses?

Most people don’t wake up thinking about opening a new bank account. Banking is often a “set it and forget it” decision—many stick with the account they opened as teenagers because switching feels like too much effort.

To overcome this laziness, banks offer attractive sign-up bonuses, like $300 or more, to encourage people to open new accounts.

The goal? Once you’re in the door, banks hope to sell you additional products like credit cards, investment accounts, or loans, making your relationship with them more profitable over time.

For those aware of these offers, bank bonuses are an easy way to create a new stream of income—if you know how to take advantage of them.

Who Can Get a Bank Bonus?

The great thing about bank bonuses is basically everyone is eligible for them!

New Customers Only:

The primary restriction for most bank bonuses is that you must be a new customer. This generally means you cannot have had the same product with the bank within a specific time frame, typically the past 12 months (though this can vary by bank). For example, if you currently have or previously held a Chase checking account within the last year, you won’t be eligible for another bonus on the same account type.

Social Security Number:

Anyone with a Social Security number is eligible to open a financial account, which is a requirement to qualify for bank bonuses.

Where To Start

When it comes to bank bonuses, there are several types of accounts that offer them: checking, savings, and brokerage accounts. However, checking account bonuses are typically the easiest and most straightforward to earn, making them the best place to start.

Why Checking Accounts Are Best

  1. Simple Requirements: Most checking bonuses only require you to set up a direct deposit of a specific amount.
  2. Low Barriers: Unlike savings or brokerage accounts, checking bonuses usually don’t require large sums of money to be held for an extended period.
  3. Higher Opportunity Cost: Savings and brokerage bonuses often tie up your funds, which could instead earn interest in a high-yield savings account or be invested elsewhere.

There are hundreds of banks across the country offering lucrative checking account bonuses, so you have plenty of opportunities to keep things simple and profitable.

How Banks Fight Bonus Hunting

Churning bank accounts, while not as well-known as credit card churning, is still a strategy many use to earn extra money through bank bonuses. Unlike credit card offers, there’s less buzz around bank account bonuses on social media or YouTube, which means banks don’t do as much to prevent people from taking advantage of these offers.

The main method banks use to deter bonus hunters is by checking your ChexSystems score before approving your application for a new account. Think of this as a credit score for your bank accounts. The ChexSystems score is used to flag individuals who have a history of overdrawing their accounts or leaving unpaid fees or opening many new accounts. However, I’ve opened many accounts without it being an issue. If you’re applying for fewer than 10–20 new accounts in a year, your ChexSystems score is unlikely to have any negative impact on your ability to earn bonuses.

Which Is Better: Credit Card Sign On Offers or Bank Bonuses?

Bank account bonuses often offer more advantages compared to credit card bonuses, for several reasons:

  1. No Spending Requirements: Unlike credit cards, you don’t have to make purchases to earn the bonus.
  2. No Impact on Your Credit Score: Opening a bank account doesn’t affect your credit score, whereas credit card applications can.
  3. More Opportunities: You can apply for and earn many more bank account bonuses in a year compared to credit cards.
  4. Higher Bonuses: Bank account bonuses tend to be larger than those offered by credit cards.

Additionally, you can typically reapply for a bonus after about a year once the account is closed, so it’s a strategy I will consider for the future.

Frequently Asked Questions About Bank Account Bonuses

Do I Need to Keep the Bank Account Open?

Once you’ve received your bonus, you can transfer all the funds out of the account. There’s no need to keep the account active. Some banks will automatically close the account once it has a $0 balance for a set period. Alternatively, you can call the bank to close the account 90 days after receiving the bonus to ensure you meet any additional requirements. It’s wise to consult the fee section on the Profitable Content page for the specific bonus to make sure there are no fees associated with the account. Some banks may charge a fee if your balance is $0, so I recommend avoiding those banks when starting bonus hunting.

How Long Does It Take to Receive the Bonus?

The time it takes to receive your bonus can vary from bank to bank. Some banks, like Chime, pay out within a week, while others may take up to 90 days after you complete the required actions. Always check the bank’s specific terms for exact payout timelines.

Is This Money Taxable

Yes, bank bonuses are considered income, so you are required to pay taxes on them. If the bonus is significant enough, the bank may send you a 1099-INT form. If you don’t receive one, you should report the bonus as other interest income on your taxes. While you may be able to skip reporting the bonus if you don’t receive the 1099-INT, I recommend including it in your tax return to avoid any issues.

Note: This is not tax advice—consult with a tax professional for personalized guidance.
