Introductory Offers Hacked: Churning Your Way to Free Money

Credit cards are notorious for offering tempting sign-up bonuses, like: “Spend $500 in the first 3 months and get $200 back.” While $200 is great, the real benefit is that many banks offer similar bonuses across multiple cards. This lets you take advantage of sign-up offers one after the other. Follow these steps to consistently earn rewards without extra spending:

  1. Apply for a card with a bonus offer, like “Spend $500 in 90 days, get $200.”
  2. Use the card for your regular expenses—things you would buy anyway—until you meet the $500 threshold. This means no extra spending; just use the card for your usual purchases.
  3. Collect your reward! Whether it’s cashback, points, or a statement credit, you’ve earned free money for doing what you already do.
  4. Stop using the card once you get the bonus. Lock the card in your app to avoid fraudulent charges, then store it away and don’t touch it again. You’ve already received your reward, so there’s no need to keep spending.
  5. Repeat with a new card and a new bonus offer. Move on to the next card, continuing the process of churning.

NOTE: Always pay off the full balance before the due date for each card to avoid interest charges.

This strategy—known as credit card churning—works well for individuals with low expenses, such as younger people. By following these steps, you can earn "free money," build your credit, and often reach the spending requirements using only your usual expenses. Just remember, don’t spend more than you normally would to reach the bonus. Avoid lifestyle inflation at all costs! Treat each card like a debit card, and you’ll be maximizing rewards without falling into debt.

Warning: Always Pay Off Your Card

Credit cards are NOT free money. Every dollar you spend must be paid back. If you don’t pay your full balance by the due date, you’ll be charged interest—and credit card interest rates are sky-high.

NEVER EVER EVER PAY INTEREST!

To avoid this, set up autopay immediately after getting your card. Link it to your bank account and choose to pay the full balance every month. If you spend more than you can afford, you’ll fall into the trap of paying interest, effectively giving your money to the bank instead of taking advantage of their rewards.

Treat your credit card like a debit card: only spend what you’d already buy with cash. If you’re not good with money or are already in debt, using a credit card could make things worse. How Banks Shield Themselves from Excessive Card Churning

Banks are well aware that some people open credit cards just for the sign-up bonuses. To protect themselves from excessive card churning, banks have introduced several rules to limit how often you can apply for and receive new cards. If you're planning to churn cards for bonuses, it's important to know these rules so you can plan your strategy.

Chase 5/24 Rule

The Chase 5/24 Rule is one of the strictest anti-churning rules. Chase will automatically decline your application if you’ve been approved for more than five credit cards (from any bank) in the past 24 months.

Because of this rule, it’s smart to prioritize Chase cards if you want to maximize your sign-up bonuses. Start by applying for the Chase cards with the best rewards, like the Chase Freedom Flex or Chase Freedom Unlimited, early in your churning journey before the 5/24 rule becomes a problem for you.

Capital One’s Restrictions

Unlike Chase, Capital One doesn’t have a formal anti-churning rule, but they’re still very selective. They’re known for rejecting applicants who have recently opened multiple cards, even if your overall credit score is good.

For example, I applied for a Capital One card with only one new credit card in the past two years and was declined. If you’ve opened multiple cards recently, your chances of approval with Capital One are slim.

If you’re interested in a Capital One card, make sure it’s one of the first cards you apply for. If you already have two or more cards, you’re likely better off focusing on other banks, as getting approved by Capital One will be much harder. Personally, I’ve never been able to get a Capital One card and probably never will.

American Express Rules

American Express has a strict policy that limits you to one credit card approval every five days. If you’re planning to apply for multiple Amex cards, you’ll need to space out your applications accordingly.

Additionally, you’re limited to being approved for a maximum of two credit cards within a rolling 90-day period. Charge cards, like the Amex Platinum or Gold, don’t count toward this limit, so you can still apply for those even if you’ve hit the 2/90 rule for credit cards.

American Express enforces a limit on the total number of credit cards you can hold at one time. As of now, most people are limited to four or five Amex credit cards, depending on your profile and relationship with the bank. Charge cards do not count toward this limit.

American Express has a once-per-lifetime rule for their welcome bonuses. This means you can only receive the welcome bonus for each specific Amex card once, even if you close the card and reapply later. Be strategic about when you apply to maximize the value of your bonuses.

Why This Matters

Frequently Asked Questions About Credit Card Intro Bonuses

How Will This Impact My Credit Score?

Your credit score is made up of different parts, as shown in the image below. Opening a new credit card or loan affects only a small part of your score.

New credit makes up 10% of your score. This includes any accounts you've recently opened or any applications for credit that required a hard inquiry. A hard inquiry happens when a lender checks your credit to decide if they’ll approve you, and it can cause your score to drop a few points for a short time.

When you open a new account, it also lowers the average age of your credit history, which makes up 15% of your score. These two parts together (new credit and credit history) can temporarily affect up to 25% of your score. On average, opening a new account drops your score by about 10 points, but this drop is temporary and usually goes away after a few months.

Over time, opening new accounts can actually help your score by giving you more credit to use, which improves other parts of your score.

If you’re planning to take out a big loan, like a mortgage or car loan, in the next year, it’s a good idea to avoid opening new accounts so your score stays as high as possible. But if you’re not planning on getting a loan soon, the small, temporary drop won’t cause any long-term problems.

Important Notes on How Using Cards and Credit Impacts Spending

Studies show that people tend to spend more money when using credit cards compared to cash. Why? Swiping or tapping a card feels far less "real" than handing over physical cash—it’s simply less mentally painful.

Don’t fall into the trap of overspending just because it’s easy. Stick to your regular budget and use your credit card only for purchases you were already planning to make. Avoid lifestyle inflation at all costs! Just because you have access to credit doesn’t mean you should start buying things you don’t need. Discipline is key to making credit work for you, not against you.

Don't Use Cards With Annual Fees

If you’re new to credit cards, it’s best to avoid cards with annual fees. These fees are charged every year just for keeping the card, and they can quickly eat into any rewards you earn. Luckily, there are plenty of excellent no-annual-fee cards that offer great rewards and perks, so there’s no need to jump into fee-based cards right away.

While some cards with annual fees do have attractive sign-up bonuses, you’re better off focusing on no-fee cards first. You can always look into annual fee cards later, once you’ve built a solid credit history and maximized the free offers. Plus, sticking to no-fee cards means you won’t have to worry about canceling the card in the future or justifying the yearly cost.
